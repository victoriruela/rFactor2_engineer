# Suspension Geometry — Knowledge Base

## Spring Rates

Springs control the **static and dynamic ride height** and determine how weight transfers between axles:

### Front Springs
- **Stiffer front springs**: Reduce nose dive under braking, improve turn-in response, but reduce front mechanical grip
- **Softer front springs**: More grip (larger contact patch variation), better bump absorption, but more dive and slower transients
- **Effect on balance**: Stiffer front → more understeer; Softer front → less understeer

### Rear Springs
- **Stiffer rear springs**: Reduce squat under acceleration, more responsive rear, but can cause snap oversteer
- **Softer rear springs**: More rear grip, better traction, but more squat and less predictable transitions
- **Effect on balance**: Stiffer rear → more oversteer; Softer rear → less oversteer

### Spring Rate Guidelines
- **GT cars**: Front 80–150 N/mm, Rear 100–180 N/mm (rear typically 10–30% stiffer)
- **Open wheelers**: Front 150–300 N/mm, Rear 180–350 N/mm
- **Front:Rear ratio**: Usually 0.70–0.90 (softer front for understeer protection)

## Ride Height

Ride height affects aerodynamics, center of gravity, and roll characteristics:

- **Lower ride height**: Lower CoG (less weight transfer), more ground effect, better aero efficiency
- **Higher ride height**: More suspension travel available, safer over curbs, less bottoming risk
- **Front ride height**: Typical range 15–40mm. Below 15mm risks bottoming.
- **Rear ride height**: Typical range 20–50mm. Below 20mm risks bottoming.

### Rake (Front-to-Rear Height Difference)
- **Positive rake** (rear higher than front): Standard for most cars. Improves front aero, aids turn-in.
- **Typical rake**: 2–8mm (rear higher)
- **Excessive rake (>10mm)**: Destabilizes rear aero, risks ground contact at front
- **Zero or negative rake**: Unusual; reduces front grip, inhibits turn-in

## Damper Tuning

Dampers control the **rate** of suspension movement, not the total travel:

### Bump (Compression) Damping
- Controls how fast the suspension compresses when hitting a bump or under weight transfer
- **Slow bump**: Affects braking dive, turn-in compression, acceleration squat
- **Fast bump**: Affects response to curbs, bumps, surface irregularities

### Rebound (Extension) Damping
- Controls how fast the suspension extends after being compressed
- **Slow rebound**: Affects recovery after weight transfer (corner exit, braking release)
- **Fast rebound**: Affects response to dips, quick direction changes

### Damper Tuning Strategy
1. **Start with bump damping**: Set slow bump to control body motion under braking and turn-in
2. **Match rebound**: Rebound typically 60–80% of bump value for same speed range
3. **Fast damping**: Set lower than slow (40–60%) to allow bump absorption
4. **Signs of over-damping**: Harsh ride, poor compliance, grip drops on bumpy surfaces
5. **Signs of under-damping**: Body oscillation, inconsistent platform, slow to settle

## Anti-Roll Bars (ARBs)

ARBs connect left and right wheels on an axle, transferring load during cornering:

### Function
- **Stiffer ARB**: More load transfer to outer wheel → less grip on that axle in corners
- **Softer ARB**: Less load transfer → more total grip on that axle, but more body roll

### Balance Effect
- **Stiffer front ARB**: Less front grip → more understeer
- **Stiffer rear ARB**: Less rear grip → more oversteer
- **Balance is RELATIVE**: Only the ratio matters. Stiffening both equally changes roll but not balance.

### Interaction with Springs
- ARBs only act in roll (lateral weight transfer). Springs act in both pitch (longitudinal) and roll.
- If springs are very stiff, ARBs have less effect (less roll to resist)
- If springs are soft, ARBs are very influential in determining balance
