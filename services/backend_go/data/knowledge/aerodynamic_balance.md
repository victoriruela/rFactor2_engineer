# Aerodynamic Balance — Knowledge Base

## Downforce Fundamentals

Downforce is the vertical load generated by aerodynamic surfaces. It increases tire grip but at the cost of aerodynamic drag:

- **Downforce ∝ speed²**: Doubling speed quadruples downforce. Aero effects are negligible below ~80 km/h and dominant above ~150 km/h.
- **Drag ∝ speed²**: Every downforce increase comes with proportional drag increase, reducing top speed.
- **Trade-off ratio**: A good aero package generates 3–5 units of downforce per 1 unit of drag (L/D ratio).

## Front/Rear Balance

The **aero balance** is the ratio of front to rear downforce, expressed as % front:

- **50/50**: Neutral aero balance. Neither axle dominates. Good baseline.
- **>55% front**: Front-heavy aero. Excess front grip at speed → high-speed oversteer tendencies.
- **<45% front**: Rear-heavy aero. Excess rear grip at speed → high-speed understeer.

**Key insight**: Aero balance ONLY affects high-speed behavior. Low-speed balance is purely mechanical (springs, ARBs, diff).

### Speed-Dependent Handling

If handling character changes with speed:
- **Understeer gets worse at higher speeds** → aero balance is too rearward (too much rear downforce relative to front)
- **Oversteer appears only at high speed** → aero balance is too forward (too much front downforce relative to rear)
- **Handling is consistent across speeds** → aero balance matches mechanical balance. Ideal state.

## Wing Adjustments

### Front Wing
- Increasing front wing angle → more front downforce → more front grip at speed
- Also increases front drag slightly
- Effect on balance: shifts aero balance forward (more % front)

### Rear Wing
- Increasing rear wing angle → more rear downforce → more rear grip at speed
- Significant drag increase (rear wing is larger surface)
- Effect on balance: shifts aero balance rearward (less % front)

### Wing Stall
Excessive wing angle (typically >8–10° depending on design) can cause airflow separation:
- Downforce actually DECREASES despite higher geometric angle
- Sudden snap behavior possible (lift/stall cycle)
- Always verify downforce increases proportionally to angle changes

## Ride Height Interaction

Aerodynamics and ride height are coupled:
- **Lower ride height** → ground effect increases → more downforce from underbody
- **But**: if ride height goes too low, underbody sealing is lost → sudden downforce reduction
- **More downforce** → suspension compresses → ride height drops → even more downforce (positive feedback loop)
- Springs must be stiff enough to prevent aero-induced bottoming

**Minimum safe ride heights**: Front 15mm, Rear 20mm (typical GT/prototype class in rF2).

## Drag vs. Lap Time

Every circuit has an optimal downforce level:
- **High-speed circuits** (Monza, Spa): Low wing angles, minimize drag for straight-line speed
- **Medium circuits** (Barcelona, Silverstone): Moderate wing, balanced approach
- **Tight circuits** (Monaco, Hungaroring): Maximum wing, corner speed is everything
- **Rule of thumb**: If top speed is >5% below targets and wing angles are high, reduce. If cornering speed is low, increase.
